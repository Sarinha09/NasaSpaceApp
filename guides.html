Worlds Beyond Our Sun

Exoplanets, or extrasolar planets, are planets that orbit stars outside our Solar System. The confirmation of the first exoplanet in 1992 opened an entirely new field in astronomy. Since then, thousands have been discovered, ranging from gas giants larger than Jupiter to rocky worlds similar to Earth.

The search for exoplanets is essential to answering some of humanity’s biggest questions: Are we alone in the universe? How do planetary systems form and evolve? Are there other Earths out there?

But why is it so difficult to find exoplanets?

Exoplanets are very far from Earth, and they are so small compared to the star they orbit that not even the most powerful modern telescopes come close to being able to see them. But if they are invisible, how are they detected?

There are a few methods scientists use to detect exoplanets:

  • Transit Method: Just as the Moon passing in front of the Sun blocks its light, causing an Eclipse. Planets outside the solar system can also cause "Eclipses" when they pass between their star and us. To detect these eclipses, scientists simply point a telescope at thousands of stars and note when one periodically lowers its brightness. This is likely an indication of a planetary eclipse.
  • Radial Velocity Method (Doppler Spectroscopy): It is possible that a planet's orbit does not pass between its star and us. In this case, an eclipse will never happen. However, it is worth remembering that just as a star's gravity pulls its planet, the planet also pulls its star, causing the star to move slightly in the sky. By detecting and measuring this movement, it is possible to predict the size of a planet relative to its star and the distance (and often even the shape) of its orbit.

The Transit Method

AstroBit AI focuses on the transit method, one of the most successful techniques. When a planet passes in front of its star (from our point of view), it blocks a small fraction of the light, causing a tiny and periodic dimming. Telescopes such as Kepler and TESS are designed to detect these drops in brightness, which serve as the signature of a potential planet.

Glossary of Astronomical Parameters

The classification of an object depends on the careful analysis of several parameters. Below is a breakdown of the features used by our AI model.

  • Orbital Period (days): The time it takes for the planet to complete one orbit.
  • Transit Duration (hours): The amount of time the star’s light dims.
  • Transit Depth (ppm): The percentage of light blocked, related to the planet’s size.
  • Planet Radius (R⊕): The estimated radius of the planet in multiples of Earth’s radius.
  • Stellar Temperature (K): The surface temperature of the star.
  • Stellar Radius (R☉): The radius of the star in multiples of the Sun’s radius.
